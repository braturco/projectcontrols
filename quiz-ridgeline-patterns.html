Debrief Module 4.1 — Lesson Review
What went wrong — and the correct path
Verbal authorizations for out-of-scope work bypass the project's change control system, contaminate the performance baseline, and create liability for the project coordinator. The correct action is always to raise a PCN first, even if the PM is unavailable — a work hold is preferable to unauthorized commitment. Retroactive change orders are administratively burdensome and can jeopardize client trust. Contingency is a PM-controlled reserve, not a coordinator-level discretionary fund.

Why this was the right call
Issuing a PCN before authorizing any work preserves the integrity of the performance baseline, creates an auditable paper trail, and keeps the PM informed. The key principles: (1) No scope commitment without written authorization. (2) PCN precedes all Change Orders — it is the early warning document. (3) Contingency is a PM reserve — a coordinator can flag it but not draw it unilaterally. Jamie's action kept the project moving with full transparency.

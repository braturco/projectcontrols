What did we learn about Project Controls Kick Off?

The most valuable thing a project controller does in Week 1 is not build a spreadsheet — it's asking the right questions before the spreadsheet exists. Setup decisions made in the first week govern data quality for the entire project. A PO that isn't in the system means invoices can't be processed correctly, it means that we might forget about outstanding costs when we go to close out project. Contingency sitting inside the base budget means it gets spent without authorization. Known, unbudgeted costs could impact how your accounting system recognizes revenue. A change log that starts in Month 6 has no record of the first five months of scope conversations. These problems are trivial to prevent but expensive to fix.

02
Pre-Project · Week 2
Setting Up the System
Building the WBS, loading budgets, defining rules of credit, and writing the PC Plan — the document that governs everything that follows

With the kickoff questions answered, Jamie spent Week 2 building the project controls infrastructure. The WBS in Oracle was first. Jamie had set up project codes before for administrative purposes — this was the same logic, applied more deliberately. The WBS needed to mirror the estimate structure closely enough that budget vs. actual comparisons were meaningful, but not be so granular that every minor task became its own reporting burden.

Nine WBS elements, matching the estimate's major discipline groupings. Each subconsultant — Ridgeline, Ray Callahan's archaeology firm, the TLU firm — in its own element so their costs could be tracked and reported separately from internal labor. The contingency, as agreed, outside the Oracle budget entirely.

The rules of credit conversation happened on Wednesday. Jamie had drafted proposals for each element based on the nature of the work — then walked them through with Sarah to confirm.

Jamie "I've drafted the rules of credit for each WBS element. For the drilling PO I'm proposing units complete — confirmed holes divided by 45, using Marcus's field log as the authoritative source, not Ridgeline's invoices. My reasoning is that this is the most objective measure available and it removes any reliance on either party's estimate of progress."
Sarah Chen "Correct. And that distinction — field log, not invoice — matters most precisely when there's a dispute. If Ridgeline ever invoices ahead of confirmed completions, I need to know. Keep it clean."
Jamie "For the IA report — WBS 1.8 — I've proposed weighted steps rather than a straight percentage. The report has five distinct phases with very different effort levels. If I use a flat percentage, someone saying '50% done' might mean the outline is written — which is maybe 10% of the actual effort. The weighted steps force the percentage to reflect where the work actually sits in the effort curve."
Sarah Chen "Right. What weights did you assign?"
Jamie "Outline at 5%, data compilation 15%, draft sections 45%, internal QC 65%, client draft 85%, regulatory submission 100%. The draft sections step is the biggest weight because that's where the bulk of the writing work lives."
Sarah Chen "Agreed. Lock those in."

On Friday of Week 2, Jamie sent Sarah the Project Controls Plan — a single page that documented how the project would be tracked, reported, and controlled for the next twelve months. It wasn't a long document. It didn't need to be. Its purpose was to create a shared, written understanding between Jamie and Sarah of exactly how controls would work — so that when something went wrong at Month 5, neither of them would be arguing about process.

Project Controls Plan — Ridgeline Environmental Assessment (one-page summary)
Reporting Cadence
Weekly: Timesheet coding review; Ridgeline field log reconciliation; change log update.
Monthly (last Friday): Full EV calculation; cost report pack prepared for Sarah's review; status report issued to client within 3 business days of Sarah's approval.

All scope changes logged in the change log on the day identified. No out-of-scope work proceeds without written authorization. Change orders set up as separate WBS elements — never merged with original scope. Change log shared with client PM (Dale Fortis) as part of monthly report package.
Escalation Thresholds
Any WBS element with CPI < 0.90 for two consecutive months: escalate to Sarah with cause analysis.
Any WBS element forecast to exceed budget by > 10%: escalate immediately.

Any potential changed conditions trigger (per methodology report baseline): escalate to Sarah same day.

Sarah reviewed the PC Plan and sent back two words: "Looks good." Jamie filed the signed version and sent a copy to Dale Fortis with a cover note explaining that the change log would be included in every monthly report package. Dale replied: "Appreciated — good to know how this is being tracked."

Jamie's thinking

The PC Plan took about three hours to write. Most of that time wasn't writing — it was thinking through the logic. What am I actually going to do every week? What am I going to look for? What do I escalate and when? What does Sarah need from me that she doesn't have time to produce herself? Once I had answers to those questions, the document was easy. And having it written down meant that in Month 5, when I needed to escalate the drilling situation, I wasn't inventing a process on the fly. The process was already agreed. I was just following it.

What This Illustrates

The PC Plan is not bureaucratic paperwork — it's a set of decisions made in advance about how you will handle situations you haven't encountered yet. Loading correct OT rates before the first timesheet posts is a five-minute task that protects twelve months of cost accuracy. Identifying travel time as a non-billable cost means 250+ hours of real firm expenditure has somewhere to live rather than quietly eroding margin. Formalizing Sarah's rough risk notes into a structured register with expected values means the contingency balance can be defended — and the gap between R-01's tail risk and the contingency is explained by the contract clause that covers it, not hidden. None of these are complicated. They're just the things that need to be right before the project starts moving.

End of Month 1
The First Monthly Report
Jamie runs the EV calculation for the first time and tries to explain what the numbers mean

Month 1 ended. It was time for the first cost report. Sarah had given Jamie a template from a previous project and explained the process: pull actuals from Oracle, apply rules of credit to get EV, compare to planned value, calculate the metrics, prepare the summary pack. Jamie sat down on the last Friday of the month with the Oracle export, a spreadsheet, and the rules of credit table.

The actuals pulled cleanly from Oracle. The planned value came from the baseline schedule. Then Jamie hit the EV column and stared at the formula for a few minutes. This was the 20% Dan had mentioned. Jamie picked up the phone.

Jamie "Dan — it's Jamie. I'm doing the first EV calc on Ridgeline. I've got the actuals from Oracle and I've got the rules of credit we set up. I understand the concept — budget times percent complete. But I'm second-guessing myself on whether I'm applying the rules correctly. Can I walk through the drilling PO with you?"
Dan Reyes "Go ahead."
Jamie "Drilling PO is $2.7M. Rule is units complete — confirmed holes divided by 45. Marcus's log shows 6 holes done. So EV is 6 over 45 times $2.7M, which is $360,000. Actuals are $362,000. So the CPI is $360K divided by $362K, which is 0.995. That seems almost too clean — like I must be doing something wrong."
Dan Reyes "You're not doing anything wrong. Clean early numbers are normal when a project is tracking well. The thing to remember is that CPI at Month 1 means almost nothing on its own — you've only completed 13% of the drilling program. What you're building is a baseline to compare against next month and the month after. The number isn't the point yet. The habit of producing it correctly every month is the point. Does it feel like you understand what the 0.995 means?"
Jamie "I think so. It means for every dollar we've spent on drilling, we've gotten 99.5 cents of work done. If it was 0.80, we'd have spent $1 and only gotten 80 cents of progress."
Dan Reyes "Exactly right. That's the whole thing. Call me when you hit something that doesn't make sense."
Jamie's thinking — after the call with Dan

Five minutes with Dan was worth an hour of reading. EV = budget × % complete per the rule of credit. That's it. The CPI shows whether you're getting what you paid for. If Marcus logged 5 holes but we spent enough for 6, the gap is right there in the number. The EV shows what you've accomplished, not just what you've spent.

Jamie's first EV report. CPI of 0.998 — almost exactly on budget. SPI of 1.000 — on schedule. The project is 9% complete and performing cleanly. Jamie prepared a one-page summary and brought it to Sarah.

Jamie "I've finished the Month 1 EV calc. CPI is 0.998 — essentially on budget. SPI is 1.0 — on schedule. EAC is projecting a $9,000 overrun if nothing changes, which is less than 0.2% of the budget. I think everything looks fine."
Sarah Chen "Good work. A few things to add to the summary. First, note which WBS elements have CPI below 1.0 even if they're minor — get in the habit of calling those out by name, not just showing the project total. Second, for every element that fired a new milestone this month, note what triggered it. Third, always include the contingency balance even when it's untouched — it should be on every report so the client can see it hasn't been drawn. And one more thing: the EAC projection at Month 1 on a 12-month project carries almost no statistical weight. Note it but don't lead with it."
Jamie "Why doesn't the EAC carry much weight yet?"
Sarah Chen "Because you've completed 9% of the project. The trend-based EAC extrapolates your current CPI to completion — but after 9% of the project, small fluctuations in actuals create large swings in the projection. A CPI of 0.998 looks almost perfect but it might be 0.85 next month and 1.05 the month after. The EAC becomes meaningful once you're past roughly 20% complete and a performance pattern is establishing itself. Before that it's noise."
Jamie's thinking

That was the most useful thing Sarah told me in the first month. I had been proud of the EAC number — it looked good and I wanted to lead with it. But she was right: it was Month 1. We'd barely started. The discipline she was teaching me was to report accurately but communicate carefully — show the number, but tell the reader how much confidence to place in it. That's harder than just running the calculation.

04
Months 1–3 · Finding the Rhythm
Learning the Rhythm
Three months of clean data, a consistent process, and understanding what "good" looks like

Months 1, 2, and 3 went smoothly. Jamie settled into a weekly rhythm: Monday, pull the prior week's timesheet postings from Oracle and check the WBS coding against the project register. Wednesday, reconcile Marcus's field log against Ridgeline's weekly progress report. Friday, update the tracking spreadsheet.

At month-end, Jamie ran the full EV calculation, prepared the status report pack for Sarah, and sat down with her to review it before it went to the client. These review sessions were the most valuable thirty minutes of each month. Jamie's instinct to flag a consistent pattern — even when the absolute number is small — is exactly the right instinct.

Month 5 · The Numbers Break
Something Is Wrong
Jamie spots the drilling overrun in the data before Sarah knows — and has to explain what $900K means

It was a Wednesday in Month 5 — mid-month reconciliation day. Jamie pulled Marcus's weekly field log and opened Ridgeline's progress report alongside it. The routine check lasted about three minutes before something stopped the process cold.

Marcus's log showed 27 holes completed. The plan through this point in the month had assumed 30 holes complete. Three holes behind schedule — not unusual. But the actuals in Oracle told a different story. Ridgeline's costs posted to date were $1,620,000 against 27 confirmed holes. At $60,000 per hole, 27 holes should cost $1,620,000. That part checked out.

But the note at the bottom of Marcus's log — written in the same matter-of-fact style as always — changed the picture entirely.

Will provide formal assessment by Friday."

Jamie's thinking

I read that note three times. "Program may require additional holes." I didn't know what varved clay was. But I knew what "additional holes" meant: additional holes at $60,000 each. I opened my tracking spreadsheet and started running numbers to understand what I was looking at.

Jamie worked through the EV calculation for the month-to-date picture and then extended it using Marcus's Friday assessment, which arrived as promised: 15 additional holes required. Jamie brought the analysis to Sarah the same afternoon.

Month 7 · An Unexpected Cost
The Clearing Stop
A client routing change in July triggers the bird breeding season restriction — and Jamie has to quantify the cost impact

CO-01 was authorized. The 14 approved clay zone holes were drilled and logged. The project was back on track. Jamie was getting comfortable with the monthly rhythm when Brad Kohut sent an email in the third week of Month 7 with a subject line that read: MBCA Issue — Alternate Crossing Access.

Jamie read it twice before understanding what it meant. Northern Corridor's routing team had requested an alternate borehole location for Watercourse Crossing 4 — 800 metres off the existing access trail. Getting the rig to that location required a new access spur. The new spur required vegetation clearing. The date was July 19th. The Migratory Birds Convention Act breeding season restriction ran until July 31st. No tree cutting was legally permitted.

Jamie pulled up the project risk register. The bird breeding season restriction was there — flagged at project setup, with an associated contract clause. A note in Brad's column: Clause 8.4 — client-caused delay due to regulatory restriction triggered by client routing decision.

Jamie ran the cost numbers before calling Sarah.

Month 9 · Finding the Error
The Reconciliation
Jamie finds a timesheet coding error during the monthly close — and what it cost to not find it sooner

Month 9. All field work was complete. The project was in the analysis and reporting phase, concentrated in WBS 1.8 — Impact Assessment and Reporting. Jamie ran the mid-month timesheet reconciliation on a Thursday afternoon, as usual. The actuals in Oracle against WBS 1.8 were $134,000. The weighted steps rule showed 30% complete — $111,000 was the expected AC for that level of progress. Jamie had $23,000 more than expected.

This was a $23,000 cost variance on a single WBS element in a single month. Not catastrophic, but not noise either. Jamie pulled the timesheet detail for WBS 1.8.

Jamie's thinking

When actuals are higher than expected, first question isn't "is there a problem" — it's "is this real cost or a coding error?" Honest mistakes happen. Someone charges IA writing to 1.8 instead of 1.2b. It's not fraud, it's just wrong, and it corrupts every EV number downstream until it's fixed.

Jamie sorted the WBS 1.8 timesheet by employee. Dr. Sorge's team had 14 hours coded to 1.8 at senior ecologist rates — $4,200. Jamie checked the description fields: "wildlife section draft," "caribou habitat writing," "ecological assessment narrative." That was environmental baseline report writing — it belonged in WBS 1.2b, not WBS 1.8. Coding error.

That accounted for $4,200 of the $23,000 gap. The rest needed a different explanation. Jamie reviewed the remaining hours and spotted something that wasn't a coding error at all — it was a scope issue. James Tran, the lead report author, had 62 hours in WBS 1.8 in the past three weeks with a consistent description: "regional cumulative effects research" and "50km buffer GIS analysis."

Jamie pulled the WBS 1.8 scope description. The original scope said "project-level effects analysis." Regional scale with a 50km buffer was not in the original scope. Jamie checked the AER guidance document on Sarah's shared drive. The current version had a new section — added eight months ago — requiring regional cumulative effects analysis for pipeline corridor EAs in the boreal zone. The original project scope predated this guidance update.

Jamie drafted a brief summary and brought it to Sarah.

The reconciliation told me what it was. And the scope check told me why. Three different tools, all pointing at the same answer. That felt like project controls working properly.

What This Illustrates

The timesheet coding error and the out-of-scope work were both found through the same habit: when actuals don't match expected cost for the progress claimed, investigate before reporting. A project controller who simply transcribes actuals from the accounting system into a report is a data courier. A project controller who checks the actuals against the rules of credit, identifies discrepancies, and traces them to their source is doing the job. The difference between those two things is what Jamie learned in Month 9.

Sarah Chen "We don't close a project when the submission goes in. Ridgeline Pipeline EA — Project Brief
30km Boreal Corridor · AER Environmental Assessment · $4.5M · 12 Months
Project Overview
Client: Northern Corridor Pipeline Ltd. — a mid-size pipeline developer proposing a new natural gas transmission line through boreal terrain northeast of Grande Prairie, Alberta.
Scope: Full Environmental and Socio-Economic Assessment (ESEA) for a proposed 30km pipeline corridor. AER regulatory submission required before client may receive construction approval. Our submission becomes part of the regulatory record.
Study area: 30km linear corridor in boreal mixed-wood forest. Terrain includes wetlands, 7 significant watercourse crossings, and variable soil conditions. Preliminary soil mapping is dated — geotech conditions should be treated as uncertain until confirmed by drilling.

Key Risks
R-01 · Primary Risk — Drilling Program
The geotech drilling program is the largest cost item (60% of BAC) and the highest-uncertainty element. Preliminary soil mapping is outdated. If actual conditions require additional boreholes, impact is $60,000 per hole. Changed conditions clause (§4.2) provides recovery — requires field documentation and written notice within 5 business days. Monitor Marcus's weekly field logs without fail.
R-02 · Secondary Risk — April 1 Clearing Constraint
All clearing must be complete before April 1 (MBCA breeding season restriction). If client routing changes require new clearing after this date, cost recovery is available under §8.4 (client-caused delay). Brad Kohut leads MBCA compliance monitoring.

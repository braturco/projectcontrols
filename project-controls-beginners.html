Module 01 — Introduction

What is Project Controls?

Project controls is the discipline of tracking, measuring, and guiding a project so it finishes on time, on budget, and within scope.

The Core Idea

Every project has three competing constraints: scope (what you're building), schedule (when it's done), and cost (what it costs). Project controls is the set of tools and habits that keep all three in balance throughout the project lifecycle.

Without controls, problems stay hidden until they become crises. With controls, you see issues early — while there's still time to fix them.

Why It Matters

Studies consistently show that projects without formal controls run over budget at twice the rate of those with even basic tracking in place. The earlier you catch a variance, the cheaper it is to fix.

The Triple Constraint

Scope

The defined work — what deliverables, features, or outputs the project must produce.

Schedule

The timeline — milestones, deadlines, and the sequence of work activities.

Cost

The budget — labour, materials, equipment, and overhead required to complete the work.

Quality

Often considered a fourth constraint — the standard the deliverables must meet.

What a Project Controls Person Actually Does

  • 1
    Establish a Baseline

    Agree on the approved scope, schedule, and budget before work starts. This is your measuring stick.

  • 2
    Collect Progress Data

    Gather weekly or bi-weekly updates: hours spent, percent complete, costs incurred.

  • 3
    Compare to the Baseline

    Calculate variances — where are we ahead, on track, or behind?

  • 4
    Forecast the Outcome

    Based on current performance, predict the final cost and finish date.

  • 5
    Report and Recommend

    Communicate findings to the team and propose corrective actions where needed.

Module 02 — Scope

Scope Management

Scope is the boundary of your project. Manage it poorly and you'll do extra work for free — a phenomenon called scope creep.

Defining Scope

Scope is defined in a Statement of Work (SOW) or a Scope of Work. This document describes what the project will deliver, what activities are included, and — critically — what is excluded.

A clear scope document prevents misunderstandings between the project team and the client before work begins.

The Golden Rule

If it's not in the scope document, it's not in the project. Any new request that falls outside the agreed scope should be handled through a formal change order — not silently absorbed.

Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)

A WBS breaks the total project scope into smaller, manageable chunks — down to individual work packages that can be planned, budgeted, and tracked independently.

LevelExampleDescription
1ProjectThe overall project (e.g., Highway Bridge Rehabilitation)
2PhaseMajor phases (Design, Construction, Commissioning)
3DeliverableKey deliverables within a phase (Foundation, Deck, Railing)
4Work PackageDiscrete tasks that can be assigned and tracked (Pour Deck Concrete)

Scope Creep

Scope creep happens when small additions accumulate without formal approval or budget. Each individual request seems minor, but together they can derail a project.

  • 1
    Recognize It

    Any request that wasn't in the original scope is potential creep.

  • 2
    Log It

    Record every out-of-scope request in an issues or change log.

  • 3
    Evaluate It

    Assess the time and cost impact before agreeing to anything.

  • 4
    Formalize It

    Issue a change order — get client signature before proceeding.

Module 03 — Schedule

Schedule Basics

A schedule translates the project scope into a timeline — assigning durations, sequencing tasks, and identifying when deliverables are due.

Key Scheduling Concepts

Milestone

A zero-duration marker for a significant project event — a deadline, approval, or handoff point.

Activity / Task

A specific piece of work with a start date, finish date, and duration.

Predecessor

A task that must finish (or start) before another task can begin.

Float / Slack

How much a task can slip without delaying the overall project end date.

The Critical Path

The Critical Path is the longest sequence of dependent tasks through the project. Any delay on the critical path delays the entire project. Tasks on the critical path have zero float.

Practical Tip

Focus your monitoring effort on critical path tasks. Non-critical tasks have float — they can slip a little. Critical tasks cannot slip at all without pushing your finish date.

Schedule Performance

At any point in the project, you compare where you planned to be with where you actually are.

Site Preparation100%
Structural Steel72% (planned: 80%)
Mechanical Systems30% (planned: 50%)

A task running behind plan has a negative schedule variance. If it's on the critical path, recovery actions — overtime, additional resources, scope reduction — must be evaluated immediately.

Module 04 — Cost

Cost Control

Cost control is about tracking spending against the approved budget and forecasting where you'll land at project completion.

Budget Structure

Original Budget (OB)

The approved budget at project start, before any approved changes.

Current Budget (CB)

The original budget plus all approved change orders to date.

Actual Cost (AC)

Money actually spent on the project so far (invoices paid, time charged).

Estimate to Complete (ETC)

How much more money is needed to finish the remaining work.

The Cost Variance Formula

Cost Variance
CV = Earned Value − Actual Cost

A positive CV means you've done more work than the money spent (good). A negative CV means you've spent more than the work is worth (over budget).

Estimate at Completion (EAC)

The EAC is your best current forecast of the final project cost. There are several ways to calculate it — the simplest is:

Estimate at Completion (simple)
EAC = Actual Cost + Estimate to Complete

Compare EAC to your Current Budget. If EAC > CB, you have a cost overrun. Report it early, explain why, and present a recovery plan.

Common Mistake

Never use "percent spent" as a proxy for "percent complete." Spending 60% of your budget doesn't mean you've done 60% of the work. Use physical progress to measure completion, and cost data to measure spending — separately.

Module 05 — Earned Value

Earned Value Management

EVM is a technique that integrates scope, schedule, and cost into a single measurement system — giving you one clear picture of project health.

The Three Core Values

Planned Value (PV)

Budget authorized for work scheduled to be done by today. Also called BCWS.

Earned Value (EV)

Budget authorized for work actually completed by today. Also called BCWP.

Actual Cost (AC)

Real cost incurred for work performed by today. Also called ACWP.

Budget at Completion (BAC)

Total approved budget for the entire project.

Key Formulas

Schedule Variance
SV = EV − PV   (negative = behind schedule)
Cost Variance
CV = EV − AC   (negative = over budget)
Schedule Performance Index
SPI = EV / PV   (<1.0 = behind)
Cost Performance Index
CPI = EV / AC   (<1.0 = over budget)

Reading the Indices

Index ValueMeaning
CPI = 1.0On budget — earning exactly what we're spending
CPI = 0.85For every $1.00 spent, only $0.85 of work is done — 15% over budget rate
CPI = 1.10For every $1.00 spent, $1.10 of work is done — ahead of budget
SPI = 0.90Progressing at 90% of planned rate — behind schedule
Module 06 — Risk

Risk Management

A risk is an uncertain event that, if it occurs, will affect your project. Managing risk means identifying these events early and planning your response before they happen.

The Risk Register

Every identified risk should be documented in a risk register — a living log that tracks each risk through the project lifecycle.

FieldDescription
Risk IDUnique identifier for tracking
DescriptionClear statement of the risk event
ProbabilityLikelihood it occurs (Low / Medium / High)
ImpactEffect on scope, schedule, or cost if it occurs
Risk ScoreProbability × Impact — used to prioritize
ResponseAvoid, Mitigate, Transfer, or Accept
OwnerPerson responsible for monitoring and response

Response Strategies

Avoid

Change the plan to eliminate the risk entirely (e.g., change a construction method).

Mitigate

Take action to reduce the probability or impact (e.g., add schedule buffer).

Transfer

Shift the risk to another party (e.g., insurance, subcontract the risky work).

Accept

Acknowledge the risk and plan a contingency if it occurs.

Contingency vs. Management Reserve

Key Distinction

Contingency is budget held for known risks — events you've identified in your risk register. Management Reserve is budget held for unknown unknowns — risks you can't yet foresee. Both are legitimate; they're controlled differently and shouldn't be mixed.

Module 07 — Reporting

Project Reporting

Reporting is how project controls delivers value to decision-makers. A good report tells a clear story: where we are, how we got here, and what happens next.

The Project Status Report

A standard status report covers five areas. Keep each section concise — executives want the headline, not the full story. Attach detail in appendices for those who need it.

  • 1
    Overall Status (RAG)

    Red / Amber / Green rating for scope, schedule, and cost. One sentence of context for each.

  • 2
    Accomplishments

    What was completed since the last report? Tie back to milestones where possible.

  • 3
    Upcoming Work

    Key activities and milestones for the next period. Flag anything at risk.

  • 4
    Issues & Risks

    Open issues requiring a decision, and top risks with current status.

  • 5
    Forecast

    Updated EAC and projected completion date. Variance from baseline with explanation.

RAG Status Explained

ColourMeaningAction Required
GreenOn track — no significant variancesContinue monitoring
AmberAt risk — variance exists but recoverableMitigation plan in progress
RedOff track — significant variance, recovery uncertainEscalate; corrective action needed
Reporting Discipline

Never hide a red status hoping it resolves itself. Reporting red early gives the team time to act. Reporting red late — or not at all — destroys trust and makes recovery nearly impossible.

Module 08 — Changes

Change Management

Changes are inevitable. The goal isn't to prevent them — it's to control them so every change is properly evaluated, approved, and reflected in the baseline.

The Change Control Process

  • 1
    Identify

    Recognize that a change has been requested or is needed. Log it immediately in the change register.

  • 2
    Evaluate

    Assess the impact on scope, schedule, cost, and risk. Be thorough — changes rarely affect just one dimension.

  • 3
    Submit

    Prepare a formal Change Order (CO) or Change Request (CR) document with the impact assessment.

  • 4
    Approve / Reject

    The appropriate authority (client, sponsor, change board) reviews and decides. Never proceed without approval.

  • 5
    Implement

    Update the schedule, budget, and scope documents to reflect the approved change. The baseline moves.

What Goes in a Change Order?

ElementDescription
CO NumberUnique sequential identifier for tracking
DescriptionClear statement of what is changing and why
Scope ImpactWhat additional (or removed) work is involved
Schedule ImpactDays added or removed from the project schedule
Cost ImpactDollar value of the change (labour, materials, OH&P)
SignaturesClient and contractor sign-off before work proceeds
The Bottom Line

A well-managed change register is proof that your team responded professionally to a changing project environment. It protects both the contractor and the client — and it's your first line of defence in any dispute.

Lessons Learned

At project close, document what drove changes — design gaps, client-directed additions, unforeseen conditions. This feeds future project estimating and planning. Good project controls closes the loop.

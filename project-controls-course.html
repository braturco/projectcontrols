Module One
PM Language for Technical People
Project managers speak a dialect you weren't taught. This module decodes it so you can operate confidently in any project meeting.
Why Language Matters

Technical professionals often know exactly what's happening on a project but struggle to communicate it in the terms clients and project managers expect. The result: you seem uncertain, even when you're not. Learning the vocabulary of project controls is not about bureaucracy — it's about credibility.

Key Insight

The fastest way to lose a client's confidence is to use technical jargon when they ask about budget status, or to give a vague answer when they ask about schedule. Precision in language signals precision in work.

The Essential Vocabulary

Project managers and clients use formal terms you won't find on your engineering degree. The table below maps each formal term to its plain English meaning and what you'll actually hear in meetings — plus why each one matters to you.

How to Use This Table

When someone says "what's the EAC looking like?" or "are we still within the not-to-exceed?", you need to know immediately what they're asking for. Fluency in this language is what separates a project controls professional from someone who just does data entry.

Formal Term Plain English What You'll Hear in Meetings Why It Matters to You
Scope The work we agreed to do "Is that in scope?" / "That's out of scope." / "Scope creep." Everything not in scope is either a change order or a gift to the client. You need to know the boundary cold.
BAC
Budget at Completion		 The total project budget "The budget." / "Our number." / "The contract value." / "The cap." This is the ceiling. Everything you track is measured against it. It only changes through an approved change order.

Actual Cost		 What we've spent so far "Actuals." / "Posted costs." / "What's hit the books." / "Spend to date." What your accounting system shows. The starting point for all cost reporting. Check that it's accurate before you report.

Earned Value		 The budget value of work we've actually completed "How much have we earned?" / "What's our earned value?" / "BCWP." This is the most misunderstood number. It's not what you've spent — it's what you've accomplished in budget terms. BAC × % complete.
PV
Planned Value		 What we planned to have done by now "Planned spend." / "The S-curve." / "BCWS." / "Where we should be." Your scheduled progress expressed in dollars. Comparing EV to PV tells you if you're ahead or behind on the work itself.
EAC
Estimate at Completion		 What we now think the total will cost "The forecast." / "Our EAC." / "Where do you think we'll land?" / "Projected final cost." The most important number in your status report. Clients and management make decisions based on your EAC. It must be defensible.
ETC
Estimate to Complete		 What it will cost to finish the remaining work "How much more do you need?" / "Remaining budget." / "What's left to spend?" A judgment call — not a formula. Ask the person doing the work, not your spreadsheet. AC + ETC = EAC.
VAC
Variance at Completion		 Projected overrun or underrun at project end "How much are we over?" / "What's the exposure?" / "The overrun." BAC − EAC. Negative means projected overrun. This is what triggers the difficult client conversation.
CPI
Cost Performance Index		 Are we getting our money's worth? "What's the CPI?" / "How efficient are we?" / "Are we burning too fast?" EV ÷ AC. Below 1.0 means you're spending more than the work is worth. A CPI of 0.85 means 15 cents of every dollar is lost efficiency.
SPI
Schedule Performance Index		 Are we keeping up with the plan? "Are we on track?" / "What's our schedule performance?" / "How behind are we?" EV ÷ PV. Below 1.0 means less work is done than planned. SPI and CPI together tell the full story.
Milestone A key completion point "When's the next milestone?" / "We missed the milestone." / "Gate review." Has no duration — it's a moment, not a task. Milestones are what clients track. Missing one is visible and serious.
Float / Slack How much a task can slip before it hurts the end date "How much float do we have?" / "Is that on the critical path?" / "How much room?" Zero float = critical path = no wiggle room. Understanding float tells you where to spend your attention on a schedule.
Contingency The rainy-day fund for known risks "What's left in contingency?" / "We need to draw on contingency." / "The risk budget." Owned by the PM, set aside for identified risks. Not a slush fund — every drawdown should be documented against a specific risk event.
Management Reserve Emergency budget for true surprises "That's above the PM's authority." / "We'd need to go to management reserve." / "The reserve." Above the PM's control. Requiring it means the project has exceeded its risk envelope. Needs senior approval.
Deliverable A specific thing we hand over "When's the deliverable due?" / "Is the report out?" / "What's the output?" Your work only counts when a deliverable is complete and accepted. Hours alone don't close a project.
Variance The gap between plan and reality "What's the variance?" / "Why are we over?" / "Explain the delta." Negative variance = worse than planned. Always diagnose: is this a real problem or just a timing issue? Context matters.
NTE
Not-to-Exceed		 The spending ceiling — do not cross it "What's the NTE?" / "Don't exceed the cap." / "Stay within the ceiling." Common on T&M contracts. Once you hit the NTE, work stops — or you've just done free work. Track your burn rate against it constantly.
Change Order (CO) A formal add-on to the contract "We need a CO for that." / "Put in a change order." / "It's a variation." The mechanism for converting out-of-scope work into paid work. No CO = no payment. Raise them early, raise them clearly.
WBS
Work Breakdown Structure		 The project organized as a numbered hierarchy "What WBS code is that under?" / "Charge it to 1.3." / "Which task number?" Everything — costs, schedule, reporting — attaches to the WBS. Using the right code is how your work gets tracked correctly.

Module Two
How Projects Are Structured
Before you can control a project, you need to understand how it's organized — the work breakdown, the budget breakdown, and the schedule hierarchy.
The Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)

The WBS is the backbone of project controls. It breaks the total scope into smaller, manageable pieces — and everything (budget, schedule, reporting) attaches to it.

  • Level 1: The whole project (e.g., "Highway Bridge Rehabilitation")
  • Level 2: Major phases or deliverable groups (e.g., "Preliminary Engineering," "Final Design," "Construction Support")
  • Level 3: Individual deliverables or work packages (e.g., "Geotechnical Report," "Structural Drawings")
  • Level 4+: Tasks within a work package (used in detailed project plans)
Rule of Thumb

Work packages at the lowest level you'll track should represent 2–8 weeks of effort and have a clear, verifiable completion criteria. Too small = overhead. Too large = no visibility into problems.

The Cost Breakdown Structure (CBS)

While the WBS organizes what work is done, the CBS organizes how costs are categorized. Common CBS categories in consulting:

Direct Labor
Hours worked by staff on billable project activities. Your primary cost driver in consulting.
Subconsultants
Third-party firms engaged to perform specific scope. Often passed through at cost + a markup.
Direct Expenses
Reimbursable costs: travel, per diem, printing, survey equipment rentals, etc.
Overhead / Indirect
Applied as a multiplier to labor — covers rent, administration, insurance, non-billable time.
Fee / Profit
The firm's margin. In lump sum, this is what's left after all costs. In cost-plus, it's a defined amount or %.
The Project Budget Matrix

In practice, you'll often see a budget that crosses the WBS with the CBS — each WBS element has a labor budget, a subconsultant budget, and an expenses budget. This is your control document.

Control Accounts & Work Packages

A Control Account is the intersection of a WBS element and a resource category — it's where you actually measure performance. Each control account has a budget, a schedule, and someone accountable.

Practical Tip

Even on small projects, assign a single person as the "owner" of each major WBS element. They are responsible for updating % complete, flagging issues, and forecasting remaining work. Without ownership, nothing gets updated.

Module Three
Cost Control Fundamentals
Cost control is not just data entry. It's the continuous act of comparing where you are to where you planned to be — tracking margin, recognizing revenue honestly, and forecasting where you'll end up.
The Three Numbers You Always Need
📊
Budget
What you planned to spend (BAC — Budget at Completion). This is your baseline. It does not change unless scope changes.
💸
Actuals
What you have actually spent to date. Labor costs, invoices received, expenses posted. Real money out the door.
🔮
Forecast (EAC)
What you now expect to spend in total by project end. This is the number clients and management care most about.
The Forecasting Mindset

Actuals tell you history. Forecasts tell you the future. Cost control is primarily about producing accurate forecasts — not just reporting what has already happened. If your EAC equals your BAC with 10% work remaining and a known risk, your forecast is wrong.

Hours: Your Primary Currency

In consulting, labor is typically 65–85% of total project cost. Every cost control conversation starts with hours. You must track:

Budgeted Hours
Hours allocated to each WBS element in the project budget. Your baseline.
Actual Hours (to date)
Hours charged to the project so far, from timesheets. Usually available weekly.
Remaining Hours (ETC)
Estimate to Complete — how many hours are needed to finish the remaining work. This is a judgment call, not a formula.
Total Forecast Hours (EAC)
Actuals + Remaining. Compare this to budgeted hours to see your variance.
The Cost Control Cycle
1
Collect Actuals
Pull posted timesheet hours and approved invoices for the reporting period. Verify data is complete and correctly coded to WBS elements.
2
Assess % Complete
For each work package, determine how much of the scope is actually done — not just how many hours you've spent. This is where technical knowledge is essential.
3
Estimate Remaining Work
Ask the person doing the work: "Setting aside what you've already spent, how many hours does it take to finish?" This is your ETC.
4
Calculate Forecast (EAC)
Add actuals + ETC for each work package. Roll up to the total project level.
5
Analyze Variances
Compare Budget vs. Forecast for each element. Investigate any element where the forecast exceeds budget by more than 10% or your threshold.
6
Take Action & Report
Implement corrective actions (scope reduction, resource reallocation, change order) and communicate status to stakeholders.
The % Complete Trap
⚠️ Scenario — The 90% Problem
A technical lead reports that a deliverable is "90% complete." Three weeks later, it's still 90% complete. Two weeks after that — still 90%. The report was never actually 90% done; it was "90% done except for the hard parts."

Rule: Never accept a % complete estimate without asking: "What specifically is done, and what specifically remains?" For deliverables, % complete should be tied to discrete sub-tasks, not intuition.

Common % complete methods for consulting deliverables:

MethodHow It WorksBest For
Units CompleteCompleted units ÷ total unitsRepetitive tasks (drawings, inspections)
Milestone GatesSet % at defined milestones (0/50/100)Short deliverables, reports
Weighted StepsEach sub-task has a weight; sum completed weightsMulti-step deliverables
Cost RatioActuals ÷ EAC (dangerous — assumes spending = progress)Avoid unless nothing else is possible
Cost, Margin & How Consulting Firms Make Money

Understanding cost control without understanding margin is like tracking speed without knowing where the speed limit is. This section explains how consulting firms price work and where the money goes — so you understand what you're actually protecting when you control costs.

How a Consulting Fee Is Built

A consulting fee is not just "hours × rate." There are multiple layers between a staff member's salary and the price a client pays. Understanding this stack is critical for cost control.

Raw Labor Cost
The actual salary cost per hour for a staff member. Example: a $90,000/yr engineer working 1,800 billable hours = $50/hr raw cost.
Overhead Rate
A multiplier applied to raw labor to cover indirect costs: rent, IT, HR, insurance, non-billable time, benefits. Typical consulting overhead: 1.4× to 1.8× raw labor. So that $50/hr engineer costs the firm ~$75–$90/hr fully loaded.
Fully Loaded Cost
Raw labor × overhead multiplier. This is what the work actually costs the firm before any fee is added.
Fee / Profit
The margin added on top of fully loaded costs. On a lump sum, this is what's left after all costs are paid. On cost-plus, it's defined contractually.
Billing Rate / Charge-Out Rate
The rate the client is charged per hour. Typically Raw Labor × a multiplier of 2.5× to 3.5×. This covers overhead + profit in a single number.
📐 Example — Following the Dollar
Engineer salary: $90,000/yr → $50/hr raw labor cost
Overhead multiplier: 1.6× → $80/hr fully loaded cost
Billing rate: 2.8× raw = $140/hr billed to client

Margin per hour = $140 − $80 = $60/hr gross margin (43%)

If that engineer spends 2 extra unbudgeted hours on a lump sum task, the firm absorbs $160 in cost with no additional revenue. At scale, this is how projects lose margin quietly.
Markup vs. Margin — The Confusion That Costs People

These two terms are used interchangeably in conversation but mean different things mathematically. Mixing them up causes real errors in budgets and fee proposals.

Markup
Markup % = (Revenue − Cost) ÷ Cost × 100
Profit expressed as a percentage of COST. "We marked up by 25%."
Gross Margin
Gross Margin % = (Revenue − Cost) ÷ Revenue × 100
Profit expressed as a percentage of REVENUE. "We made 20% margin." This is the standard financial metric.
CostRevenueMarkup %Gross Margin %What people say
$80$10025%20%"25% markup" = "20% margin" — same deal, different math
$80$9620%16.7%Common confusion: "20% markup" is NOT "20% margin"
$80$12050%33.3%Strong margin project
Watch Out For

If someone says "we need to make 20% on this project" — ask them: 20% of what? 20% markup on cost is very different from 20% gross margin on revenue. The difference on a $500,000 project is $16,667 of profit.

Gross Margin vs. Net Margin

Gross margin measures project-level profitability. Net margin measures firm-level profitability after all overhead and G&A is absorbed.

Gross Margin
(Revenue − Direct Project Costs) ÷ Revenue. This is what the project contributes before firm overhead is allocated. A healthy consulting project targets 30–45% gross margin.
Net Margin
(Revenue − All Costs including overhead) ÷ Revenue. This is the firm's bottom line after everything is paid. Healthy professional services firms target 10–20% net margin.
DLM — Direct Labour Multiplier

The Direct Labour Multiplier (DLM) is one of the most useful efficiency metrics in consulting project controls. It measures how much net revenue the firm generates for every dollar of raw labor cost.

Direct Labour Multiplier (DLM)
DLM = Net Revenue ÷ Raw Labour Cost
Net Revenue = total contract revenue minus subconsultant and direct expense pass-throughs (i.e., revenue the firm actually earns on its own effort).

Why subtract subconsultants and expenses first? Because those dollars pass through the firm with minimal effort — the firm didn't "earn" them through labor. DLM isolates how efficiently the firm converts its own staff hours into revenue.

📐 DLM Example — Why It Matters
Contract value: $200,000
Subconsultant costs: $40,000 | Direct expenses: $10,000
Net Revenue = $200,000 − $40,000 − $10,000 = $150,000
Raw Labour Cost (actual salaries): $55,000

DLM = $150,000 ÷ $55,000 = 2.73

Interpretation: For every dollar of raw salary cost, the firm is generating $2.73 in net revenue. Typical targets range from 2.5× to 3.5× depending on the firm and contract type. A DLM below 2.0 usually signals a problem — either the project is being over-serviced, rates are too low, or too much overhead is being absorbed.
DLM RangeWhat It SignalsCommon Causes
< 2.0Losing money or barely covering overheadScope creep, under-staffed rates, fixed price bid too low
2.0 – 2.5Marginal — covering costs, thin profitCompetitive bid, high pass-throughs eating net revenue
2.5 – 3.5Healthy — firm target zoneWell-estimated project, controlled scope
> 3.5Strong — efficient delivery or conservative bidJunior-heavy team, well-scoped T&M, change orders captured
DLM as an Early Warning Tool

Track DLM monthly during execution, not just at the end. If your DLM is trending down — net revenue flat but labor costs rising — you are burning your margin in real time. A declining DLM is the first signal that hours are being spent without corresponding scope value.

How Margin Erodes — The Six Silent Killers

Projects rarely blow their budget in one dramatic event. Margin is usually lost in small, repeatable ways that seem harmless in isolation but compound into significant overruns. Every person on a project team is responsible for recognizing these patterns.

1
Scope Creep Without Change Orders
The most common cause. Work is added informally — extra meetings, additional analysis, revised deliverables — and never converted to a change order. Each individual instance seems too small to bother with. Cumulatively, it can consume an entire project's contingency.
2
Over-Servicing
Senior staff spend time on tasks budgeted for junior staff. A principal reviewing every memo, a senior engineer doing drafting work. The hours match the budget but the cost doesn't — senior rates are higher, eroding margin even when the hour count looks fine.
3
Rework
Redoing work due to errors, poor briefs, or changed client direction. Rework hours are real costs with zero progress value. On a fixed price contract, every rework hour comes directly off the firm's margin.
4
Client-Caused Delays (Unclaimed)
Client review cycles extend, data arrives late, approvals stall. The project team keeps running — coordination, chasing, re-mobilizing — and absorbs those costs without claiming the delay. These are often billable but require documentation and assertiveness to recover.
5
Late Closeout
Hours continue to be charged to a project after all substantive work is done. Administration, project management, chasing sign-offs. The project is "99% done" for weeks. These hours have no offsetting revenue on a fixed price contract.
6
Underbidding the Original Estimate
The project was bid to win, not to make money. No amount of cost control can save a project that was priced below cost from the start. This is a pre-award problem — but the project controls team is often the first to discover it and must escalate immediately.
Rules of Credit — What They Are and Why They Matter

If you've ever been asked "what rule of credit are you using for that work package?" and had no idea what that meant — this section is for you. Rules of credit are one of the most practical tools in project controls, and once you understand them, you'll use them constantly.

The Problem They Solve

Imagine you have 50 deliverables in a project. At month-end, you need to report how much of the total work is complete. Asking each person "what percentage are you done?" and relying on their gut feel produces inconsistent, unreliable numbers — the 90% problem in practice across an entire portfolio.

A rule of credit is a pre-agreed, objective rule for how a work package earns its % complete. It removes judgment from the measurement and makes progress tracking consistent, auditable, and defensible. You set the rule at the start of the project — before work begins — and apply it mechanically throughout.

The Key Principle

A rule of credit answers one question: "Under what specific conditions does this task get credit for being X% complete?" The answer must be observable and verifiable by someone who didn't do the work.

The Common Rule Types
Rule TypeHow It WorksBest ForWatch Out For
0 / 100 No credit until the task is 100% complete. Binary — done or not done. Short tasks (1–2 weeks), single-output deliverables, tasks where "almost done" has no value Can make progress look stalled if many tasks are in-flight simultaneously — use only for short work packages
50 / 50 50% credit when the task starts (or reaches a defined start gate), 100% when complete. Medium-duration tasks (2–6 weeks) where start and finish are clear events The "50% at start" credit can be gamed — define the start gate precisely (e.g., "first draft issued for review," not "work has begun")
25 / 75 25% credit at a defined start milestone, 75% remaining at completion. Tasks where early-stage effort is small but you want to acknowledge mobilization Less common; ensure the milestones are meaningful checkpoints
Milestone Gates Credit is awarded at defined checkpoints. Example: 20% on outline approval, 60% on draft submission, 100% on final acceptance. Phased deliverables — reports, design packages, studies with review cycles Milestones must be defined upfront with clear, objective completion criteria. Vague milestones defeat the purpose.
% Complete (Subjective) The responsible person estimates % complete each period, within defined bounds (often capped at 80% until officially done). Long-duration tasks where interim progress is real and meaningful Susceptible to the 90% problem. Use only when other rules are impractical, and cap the maximum reportable % (e.g., no more than 90% until deliverable is issued).
Units Complete Completed units ÷ total units. If 30 of 100 drawings are issued, you're 30% complete. Repetitive, countable work: drawings, inspections, boreholes, data records, calculations Ensure unit definitions are consistent — a "completed drawing" should mean the same thing for all units
Weighted Steps Sub-tasks are assigned weights totalling 100%. Each completed sub-task contributes its weight to overall %. Complex deliverables with distinct, unequal sub-phases Weight assignments must be agreed upfront. Re-weighting mid-project is a red flag.
How Rules of Credit Feed into Earned Value

Rules of credit are the mechanism by which % complete becomes Earned Value (EV). This is the critical link — without a rule of credit, your EV is just someone's opinion.

Earned Value from Rules of Credit
EV = BAC (work package) × % Complete (per rule of credit)
The rule of credit determines the % complete. The budget determines the scale. The result is your EV — an objective measure of progress in dollar terms.
📐 Example — 50/50 Rule in Practice
Work package: "Geotechnical Report — Draft" | Budget: $12,000 | Rule: 50/50

Month 1: Work has started but draft not yet issued. Per the 50/50 rule, no credit yet — the start gate hasn't been hit.
EV = $0

Month 2: Draft report issued to client for review. Start gate hit. EV = 50% × $12,000 = $6,000

Month 3: Final report accepted. Task complete. EV = 100% × $12,000 = $12,000

No subjectivity. No "I think we're about 70% done." The rule makes the number.
The Smoothing Effect — Why Errors Cancel Out at Scale

Here's a reassuring truth about rules of credit that many people don't realize: when applied consistently across many work packages, individual measurement errors tend to cancel out at the project level.

Think about it this way. At any given point in a project, some work packages will have their rule of credit firing slightly early (over-claiming progress) and some will fire slightly late (under-claiming). If your rules are consistently applied and your work packages are reasonably sized, these over- and under-claims balance each other across the portfolio.

📊 Scenario — The Portfolio Smoothing Effect
You have 40 work packages, all using 50/50 rules. At month-end:

• 8 tasks are in progress but haven't hit the 50% gate yet → EV is understated for these
• 6 tasks have hit the 50% gate but are barely started → EV is slightly overstated for these
• 26 tasks are faithfully reflecting their true state

At the project level, the under-claims and over-claims partially offset. The aggregate EV is a reasonable approximation of true project progress — much better than 40 subjective estimates from 40 different people.

The rule matters more than the precision of any single measurement. Consistency is what makes EVM work.
What Goes Wrong When the Wrong Rule Is Applied
  • Using % Complete (subjective) on short tasks: A 1-week task reported as "70% done" for two weeks is just the 90% problem at a smaller scale.
  • Using 0/100 on long-duration tasks: A 3-month analysis task shows zero EV for 11 weeks, then suddenly jumps to 100%. This makes the project look worse than it is for most of its life, masking real progress until the end.
  • Changing rules mid-project: If you switch from 50/50 to milestone gates partway through, historical EV comparisons become meaningless. Changing rules is a form of re-baselining and must be documented and approved.
  • Defining gates vaguely: "Work has started" is not a gate. "First draft issued to the PM for internal review" is a gate. The difference is whether a third party can verify it without asking the person doing the work.
Revenue Recognition — How Your Firm Records Money Earned

You've been tracking costs all module. Now it's time to understand the other side of the ledger: how and when your firm recognizes the revenue those costs are generating. This matters to you as a project controls professional because your % complete estimates are not just an operational tool — they directly drive what your firm reports as revenue on its financial statements.

What Revenue Recognition Actually Means

When your firm wins a $200,000 contract, it does not record $200,000 in revenue on day one. Revenue is recognized as work is performed — period by period — because the firm hasn't yet earned that money until the work is done. This is the matching principle in accounting: revenue is matched to the costs incurred to earn it.

For long-term service contracts, the standard method is the Percentage of Completion (PoC) method — and it's almost certainly what your firm uses if it runs Oracle Projects, Deltek, BST, or any major professional services ERP.

PoC via Cost-on-Cost — How Oracle Calculates It

The cost-on-cost method is the most common PoC approach in engineering and consulting. The logic is simple: the percentage of the project that is complete equals the percentage of the total expected costs that have been incurred.

PoC% (Cost-on-Cost Method)
PoC% = Costs Incurred to Date ÷ Estimated Total Cost (EAC)
Recognized Revenue for the period = Contract Value (BAC) × PoC% — previously recognized revenue
What Oracle Does Each Period

At each accounting period close, Oracle Projects recalculates PoC% using the latest actuals and your current EAC. It then determines how much cumulative revenue should have been recognized to date, compares that to what has already been posted, and records the difference as the period's revenue. This happens automatically — but only if your EAC is accurate.

📐 Worked Example — PoC Revenue Recognition Over Three Periods
Contract Value (BAC): $200,000 | Original EAC: $200,000

Month 1:
Costs incurred to date: $30,000
EAC (current estimate): $200,000
PoC% = $30,000 ÷ $200,000 = 15%
Cumulative revenue to recognize: 15% × $200,000 = $30,000
Revenue recognized this month: $30,000

Month 2:
Costs incurred to date: $80,000
EAC revised upward to $220,000 (cost overrun detected)
PoC% = $80,000 ÷ $220,000 = 36.4%
Cumulative revenue to recognize: 36.4% × $200,000 = $72,700
Less previously recognized: $30,000
Revenue recognized this month: $42,700

Month 3:
Costs incurred to date: $140,000
EAC still $220,000
PoC% = $140,000 ÷ $220,000 = 63.6%
Cumulative revenue to recognize: 63.6% × $200,000 = $127,300
Less previously recognized: $72,700
Revenue recognized this month: $54,600

Key observation: When the EAC went up in Month 2, the PoC% actually decreased relative to what it would have been — recognizing less revenue per dollar of cost. This correctly signals that the firm is working less efficiently.
When PoC% and EV% Diverge — What It Signals

You now have two measures of project progress running in parallel: your EV% (Earned Value % complete) from your project controls system, and your PoC% (cost-on-cost) from your financial system. In a healthy, well-estimated project, these numbers should be close. When they diverge significantly, something is wrong — and knowing which way they diverge tells you what the problem is.

Situation EV% vs PoC% What It Signals What To Do
Aligned EV% ≈ PoC%
(within ~5%)		 Project is performing as estimated. Cost efficiency matches progress claims. Financial and operational views agree. No action required. Continue monitoring.
EV% > PoC% e.g. EV says 60% done, PoC says 45% You are claiming more progress than your costs support. Either % complete is overstated (optimism bias), or the EAC is too high (making PoC% artificially low). Risk: over-claiming progress to the client while financials show less. Review % complete estimates — are rules of credit being applied correctly? If EAC is overstated, revise it. Do not inflate EV to match invoicing milestones.
EV% < PoC% e.g. EV says 40% done, PoC says 55% Your costs are running faster than your progress claims. Either the EAC is too low (underestimating remaining work) or work is inefficient — spending without equivalent progress. Risk: revenue is recognized based on cost burn, but the project isn't actually that far along. Reassess ETC — is remaining work being underestimated? Investigate cost efficiency. Update EAC immediately. This is an early overrun signal.
Large gap either way Divergence > 10–15% A systemic issue: either the rules of credit aren't being applied, the EAC is stale, or someone is managing % complete to look good rather than reflect reality. Escalate. A large, persistent divergence is a financial reporting risk — not just a controls issue. Finance and the PM need to align.
How to Fix a Divergence
A
Fix the EAC First
Most divergences trace back to a stale or inaccurate EAC. If your EAC hasn't been updated since the project started, PoC% is being calculated against a fiction. Update EAC to the best current estimate of total project cost — this recalibrates PoC% automatically in Oracle.
B
Audit Your % Complete Estimates
Go work-package by work-package. Are rules of credit being applied consistently? Is anyone self-reporting % complete without reference to their rule? Reset any packages where the rule hasn't been followed.
C
Do Not Adjust % Complete to Match Revenue Targets
This is the critical integrity rule. You will sometimes feel pressure to "get the revenue up" by boosting % complete. Resist it. Inflated % complete corrupts your EV, produces false CPI/SPI, and eventually forces a painful correction. Report what is true — the financial consequences of a bad project should be visible, not hidden.
D
Communicate the Divergence
If EV% and PoC% are materially different after you've corrected the inputs, document the variance and its cause in your status report. Finance needs to know, and so does the PM. A surprise at year-end is far worse than a documented issue in month three.
The Risk of Over-Recognizing Revenue Early

If costs run fast early (front-loaded spending) and PoC% is based purely on cost-on-cost, the firm can recognize substantial revenue in early periods — only to find the project stalls and under-delivers later. This means revenue recognized in Q1 must be reversed in Q3. Revenue reversals are painful for the firm's financials and deeply uncomfortable for everyone involved. Accurate EAC and honest % complete estimates prevent this. The rule: never let financial pressure drive project reporting.

Earned Value Management
EVM is the industry standard for measuring project performance. It integrates scope, cost, and schedule into a single framework — and once you understand it, you'll wonder how anyone managed projects without it.

The Three Core Values

EVM works by tracking three numbers simultaneously for every project. On their own, each number is incomplete. Together, they tell the full story of project health.

PV — Planned Value
The budgeted cost of work planned to be done by now. Also called BCWS (Budgeted Cost of Work Scheduled). Think of it as your target on the S-curve — where you should be today if everything went to plan.
EV — Earned Value
The budgeted cost of work actually completed. Also called BCWP. This is your actual progress expressed in dollars — not what you spent, but what you accomplished. EV = BAC × % Complete (per your rules of credit from Module 03).
AC — Actual Cost
The actual cost incurred for all work done to date. Also called ACWP. Real money out the door — labor posted, invoices approved, expenses processed.
The Key Insight

EVM separates "how much have we spent?" (AC) from "how much should we have spent for what we've completed?" (EV). The gap between them is your cost efficiency. Without EV, you can't tell if spending $200K on a $500K project is good or bad — it depends entirely on how much work you've actually done.

The Four Key Metrics

From PV, EV, and AC you derive four metrics that tell you everything about project health in a single glance.

Cost Variance (CV)
CV = EV − AC
Positive = under budget for completed work. Negative = over budget. CV < 0 means you've spent more than the work is worth.
Schedule Variance (SV)
SV = EV − PV
Positive = ahead of schedule (more done than planned). Negative = behind schedule. Note: SV is expressed in dollars, not days.
Cost Performance Index (CPI)
CPI = EV ÷ AC
CPI = 1.0: on budget. <1.0: over budget — getting less than $1 of work per $1 spent. >1.0: under budget. The most important single number in EVM.
Schedule Performance Index (SPI)
SPI = EV ÷ PV
SPI = 1.0: on schedule. <1.0: behind schedule — completing less work than planned. >1.0: ahead of schedule.
Reading the CPI/SPI Matrix

CPI and SPI together tell a richer story than either alone. Learn to read the combination:

CPISPIWhat It MeansUrgency

The S-Curve — Visualizing Project Progress

An S-curve plots cumulative cost (or progress) against time. It gets its name from the characteristic shape of project spending: slow start as the team mobilizes, rapid acceleration through the middle, then tapering as work completes. EVM gives you three S-curves running simultaneously — and the gaps between them tell the story.

Reading the S-curve at the status date (today): if AC sits above EV, you are spending more than you're earning — cost overrun. If EV sits below PV, you have completed less work than planned — schedule delay. The ideal: all three curves converge.

The S-Shape Explained

Projects spend slowly at the start (mobilizing, planning, early deliverables), accelerate sharply through the middle phase when most work is being executed, then taper at the end as deliverables finalize and close-out begins. A project spending money faster than the S-curve predicts in early months is a warning sign — work may not be matching the spend.

Forecasting with EVM

EVM's real power is forecasting — using current performance to project where the project will end up. There are two primary methods, and knowing when to use each is critical.

EAC — Trend-Based (CPI Forecast)
EAC = BAC ÷ CPI
Assumes current cost efficiency will continue for the rest of the project. Use when the root cause of variance is systemic — the whole project is running inefficiently.
EAC — Revised Bottom-Up Estimate
EAC = AC + ETC
Use when you have a new, bottoms-up estimate of remaining work. More accurate when conditions have changed and past performance is no longer representative.
Variance at Completion (VAC)
VAC = BAC − EAC
Negative = projected overrun. This is the number that goes in your status report and triggers client conversations.
To-Complete Performance Index (TCPI)
TCPI = (BAC − EV) ÷ (BAC − AC)
The cost efficiency you must achieve on ALL remaining work to finish within budget. TCPI > 1.2 is generally considered unrealistic — the budget is gone.
Which EAC Method to Use

Use BAC ÷ CPI as your first pass — it's the mathematically honest answer based on actual performance. Then use AC + ETC when you have a credible, bottom-up re-estimate. If the two numbers differ significantly, you need to understand why before reporting either. Never choose the lower one simply because it looks better.

Ridgeline Month 5 — Sarah forecasts the overrun two ways ▼

Sarah needs to produce an EAC for the project. She runs both methods to compare.

Sarah "The overrun is real and it has a clear cause — changed site conditions on the drilling. That's not our fault and it's potentially billable as a changed conditions claim. Before I tell Dale we're $37K over, I need to talk to contracts about whether we have grounds for a change order."

This is the distinction between forecasting the financial reality and determining who pays for it. The EAC tells you what it will cost. The contract and the change management process determine who absorbs it.

EVM and Revenue Recognition — The Connection

In Module 03 you learned that Oracle calculates PoC% using cost-on-cost: costs incurred ÷ EAC. Your EVM work directly feeds this calculation. When you update your EAC in the project system, you are not just updating a forecast number — you are changing what revenue Oracle will recognize this period.

Your EVM ActionEffect on PoC%Effect on Revenue This Period

Decrease EAC (project running lean)PoC% increasesRevenue recognized increases

The Practical Consequence

When Sarah updates the Ridgeline EAC from $500,000 to $537,000, Oracle will recalculate PoC% downward — and recognize less revenue than it would have at the original EAC. This hits the period's revenue line. Finance needs to know this is coming before month-end close, not after. Updating your EAC late is not just a project controls failure — it's a financial reporting problem.

When NOT to Trust EVM — Its Limitations

EVM is powerful but not magic. Knowing its limitations prevents you from using it as a crutch or reporting false confidence.

!
EVM is only as good as your % complete estimates
If rules of credit aren't being applied consistently, EV is fiction. A CPI of 1.02 built on inflated % complete estimates is not a healthy project — it's a hidden overrun. Garbage in, garbage out. EVM amplifies the quality of your inputs, good or bad.
!
SPI becomes meaningless at project end
As a project nears completion, EV approaches BAC regardless of actual schedule performance — because EV is capped at BAC. SPI therefore drifts toward 1.0 at the end of every project, masking real schedule delays. Use actual milestone dates, not SPI, to assess schedule performance in the final 20% of a project.
!
CPI trend forecasting assumes the problem continues
EAC = BAC ÷ CPI assumes your current inefficiency will persist. If the overrun has a specific, isolated cause that has been resolved, a bottom-up ETC is more accurate. Using CPI-based EAC after a problem has been fixed will over-forecast the overrun — and cause unnecessary alarm.
!
EVM doesn't tell you why — only that something is wrong
A CPI of 0.85 is a signal, not a diagnosis. It tells you costs are outpacing progress. It doesn't tell you if that's scope creep, rework, over-staffing, a bad estimate, or a subconsultant issue. EVM points you at the problem — investigation determines the cause.
!
EVM on very small work packages creates noise
A $2,000 work package with a 50/50 rule will show wild swings in EV — $0 one period, $2,000 the next. At the project level this averages out (the smoothing effect from Module 03), but don't try to read CPI/SPI on individual small work packages in isolation.
Explaining EVM to Clients — Without the Acronyms

Most clients don't know what CPI means. But they absolutely understand the concepts behind it. Here's how to translate your EVM metrics into plain language that lands in any meeting.

What you calculatedWhat to say to the client
CPI = 0.91 "For every dollar we're spending, we're completing about 91 cents of work. We're slightly less efficient than planned — we're tracking to finish about 10% over budget if the current trend holds."
SPI = 0.87 "We've completed about 87% of the work we planned to have done by this point. We're running a few weeks behind the baseline schedule."

The Golden Rule of Client Communication

Never lead with the metric. Lead with the meaning. "Our CPI is 0.91" means nothing to most clients. "We're tracking 10% over budget because of an unexpected site condition, and here are three options" means everything.

Common EVM Mistakes — First-Timers
MistakeWhat It Looks LikeWhy It's Wrong

Module Five
Schedule Control
Schedule slippage is the most common way projects fail. This module covers how to read, maintain, and defend a project schedule as a non-scheduler.
Schedule Hierarchy

Not all schedules are equal. On consulting projects, you'll encounter multiple levels:

Level 1 — Master
High-level milestones only. 1 page. Used in client contracts and executive reports.
Level 2 — Summary
Major work packages and key deliverables. Used in monthly status reports and client meetings.
Level 3 — Detailed
Individual activities with durations, predecessors, and resource assignments. Used by the team for day-to-day work management.
Critical Path — What It Actually Means

The critical path is the longest chain of dependent activities in the schedule. Any delay on the critical path delays the project end date by the same amount.

Critical Principle

Activities NOT on the critical path have float — they can slip by their float amount without affecting the end date. Critical path activities have zero float. This is where your attention should focus.

Common errors made by non-schedulers:

  • Focusing only on the longest-duration tasks (these may have lots of float)
  • Ignoring dependencies — a 1-day task with no float matters more than a 3-week task with 2 weeks of float
  • Not updating the schedule — a schedule that isn't maintained tells you nothing
  • Confusing planned dates with committed dates (the baseline)
Schedule Health Indicators
IndicatorHealthyWarningAction Required
SPI0.95 – 1.050.85 – 0.94< 0.85
Critical Path Float0 (by definition)Approaching criticalActivities going critical unexpectedly
Late ActivitiesNone or trivial< 5% of activities> 10% of activities late

Recovery Planning

When the schedule is behind, there are only a few real options. Don't promise recovery without a plan:

A
Crashing
Add resources to critical path activities to reduce duration. Costs more. Only effective on activities where more hands actually help.
B
Fast-Tracking
Overlap activities that were planned sequentially. Increases risk of rework. Only works where the sequence was conservative, not mandatory.
C
Scope Reduction
Reduce or defer scope to hit the date. Requires client agreement. Often the most honest option.
D
Extension Request
Formally request a schedule extension from the client. Do this early — clients accept slippage better when told proactively.

Module Six
Scope & Change Management
Scope creep is the silent budget killer. This module teaches you to recognize it, document it, and convert unauthorized work into approved change orders.
What Is Scope Creep?

Scope creep is the gradual expansion of project work beyond the contracted scope — usually without corresponding increases in budget or schedule. It is almost always unintentional, and almost always comes from a desire to be helpful.

📋 Scenario — The Friendly Extra
A structural engineer is engaged to assess three bridges. During a site visit, the client asks, "While you're out there, could you just take a quick look at the culvert too?" The engineer, wanting to be helpful, says yes. This culvert review takes 6 hours, generates findings, and eventually requires a supplemental memo. Total scope creep: ~$2,000 of unrecovered cost, plus a precedent that the engineer will do extra work on request.
The Rule

There is no such thing as "quick." Every informal request costs something. Your job is to quantify it, document it, and determine whether it's an authorized change or a business development decision — before you do the work.

The Change Management Process
1
Identify the Change
Recognize when a request falls outside the contracted scope. Compare to the contract scope statement and WBS. If in doubt, ask your PM.
2
Document It Immediately
Write a brief Change Request (CR) describing: what the change is, why it's out of scope, and a preliminary estimate of cost and schedule impact.
3
Get Approval Before Starting
Work should not start on a change until it is approved — either through a formal change order, or a written authorization from the client PM. Email confirmation is sufficient.
4
Update the Baseline
An approved change order becomes a baseline revision — the budget, schedule, and scope baseline are updated accordingly.
5
Track Separately
Change order work should be tracked in its own WBS element so its cost can be reported separately and verified against the approved amount.

Module Seven
Risk & Contingency
Every project has unknowns. This module covers how to identify them systematically, price them intelligently, and manage them without drama.
Risk vs. Contingency vs. Uncertainty
Risk (Known Unknowns)
Events you can identify in advance that might happen. You can estimate their probability and impact. Example: "The client may request an additional meeting, adding 4 hours of PM time."
Contingency
Budget set aside to cover identified risks if they occur. Typically 5–15% of project budget on consulting projects. Released by the PM as needed.
Uncertainty (Unknown Unknowns)
Things you can't anticipate. Covered by Management Reserve, not contingency. Usually held at the portfolio or business unit level.
The Risk Register

A risk register is simply a table of identified risks with their attributes. Even a basic one improves project outcomes. Minimum fields:

Risk IDDescriptionProbabilityImpact ($)Expected ValueResponseOwner
R-01Client delays review by >2 weeksMedium (40%)$3,500$1,400Track, notify client proactivelyPM
R-02Additional boreholes requiredLow (20%)$8,000$1,600Contract allowance clauseGeotech Lead
R-03Key staff unavailable during crunchMedium (35%)$4,000$1,400Pre-identify backup staffPM
Total Expected Value (Contingency Basis)$4,400
Sizing Contingency

Sum the expected values of your top risks as a minimum contingency. Add a buffer for unknown risks based on project complexity and your firm's history on similar work. On a lump-sum project, contingency protects your margin. Don't give it away in scope creep.

Risk Response Strategies
🚫
Avoid
Change the plan to eliminate the risk entirely. Example: don't subcontract to a firm with a history of delays.
📤
Transfer
Shift the risk to another party — insurance, subconsultant contract clauses, client assumption of risk.
⬇️
Mitigate
Reduce probability or impact. Example: get client data early to reduce the risk of rework from outdated inputs.
✅
Accept
Acknowledge the risk and set aside contingency to deal with it if it occurs. Appropriate for low-impact or unavoidable risks.
Tracking Contingency Usage

Contingency is a controlled budget. Every time it's used, it should be logged. A simple contingency log:

  • Track each drawdown: date, amount, reason (which risk occurred), approval
  • Report contingency remaining in all status reports
  • When contingency drops below 50% of original, flag to management
  • Never use contingency for scope creep — that's what change orders are for

Writing the Narrative

Numbers need context. The written narrative in a status report should follow this structure:

1
Overall Status (RAG)
State the project status: Green (on track), Amber (at-risk, action in progress), Red (off track, significant intervention required). Be honest.
2
Accomplishments This Period
Specific deliverables completed, milestones achieved. Not "the team worked hard on design" — something measurable was done.
3
Issues & Variances
What is off-plan, why, and what the impact is. No blame — just facts and causality.
4
Actions & Decisions Needed
What you are doing to address issues. What you need from the client to keep the project moving.
5
Look Ahead
What will happen next period. Key upcoming milestones and expected deliveries.
Golden Rule of Reporting

Never let a client be surprised by a report. If the project is going red, you should have already told them verbally before the report lands. Reports confirm — they don't break news.

Delivering Bad News

This is the most important communication skill a project controls professional can develop. Follow these principles:

Non-Negotiable

Tell them early, tell them the truth, and come with a plan. Clients can accept problems. They cannot accept surprises, dishonesty, or passivity.

1
Lead with the headline
"We are projecting a cost overrun of approximately $8,000 on WBS 1.3." Don't bury it. Don't spend three paragraphs explaining before you get to the point.
2
Explain the cause clearly
"The overrun is driven by unanticipated complexity in the geotechnical model, requiring additional analysis iterations." Be specific. Vague causes feel like excuses.
3
State the impact
Quantify cost and schedule impact clearly. "This adds $8,000 and 2 weeks to the current forecast."
4
Offer options
Present 2–3 paths forward. Never present a problem without at least one solution. This keeps you in the role of advisor, not bearer of bad news.
5
Get a decision and document it
Whatever the client decides, confirm it in writing (email is fine). "As discussed, you have authorized Option 2 — we will proceed accordingly."
The Communication Log

Maintain a brief log of all significant project communications — especially decisions, approvals, and scope discussions. This protects both parties and reduces disputes.

Cover Yourself

If a client tells you verbally to proceed with out-of-scope work, follow up with an email: "Confirming our conversation — we will proceed with X as discussed. This work falls outside our original scope and will be tracked as a potential change order pending your formal authorization." This is not aggressive; it's professional.

Module Ten
Surviving the Project Handoff
Closeout is where firms leave money on the table, accumulate unbilled costs, and damage relationships. This module covers how to close cleanly.
Why Closeout Goes Wrong

Most project failures don't happen in the middle — they happen at the end. The reasons are almost always the same:

  • Unbilled change orders that were never formalized
  • Retainage held by clients because deliverables are technically incomplete
  • Hours continuing to be charged after the scope is done
  • No defined "done" — deliverables with no acceptance criteria
  • Lessons learned never captured, so the same problems recur
The Project Closeout Checklist
1
Confirm Deliverable Acceptance
Get written (or email) confirmation from the client that all deliverables are accepted. If there are outstanding comments, define what "complete" looks like and a deadline.
2
Process All Change Orders
Identify any work done outside the original scope that hasn't been formalized. Issue change orders or document write-offs with management approval.
3
Final Invoice and Accounts Receivable
Issue the final invoice promptly upon acceptance. Follow up on any outstanding invoices. Release of retainage (if applicable) requires formal written acceptance.
4
Close the Project in Your Systems
Mark the project closed in your accounting system to stop timesheet charges. Archive project files according to your firm's retention policy and contract requirements.
5
Final Cost Report
Produce a final budget vs. actual report. Document variances. This becomes the basis for estimating similar future projects.
6
Lessons Learned
Brief session with the team: what went well, what didn't, what would we do differently? Document key findings and link them to the final cost report.
Protecting Your Firm's Interests
Common Mistake

Technical staff continue charging hours to a project after their scope is done, simply because the project hasn't been formally closed. This destroys margin. Once your deliverable is complete and accepted, stop charging — or get an explicit authorization to continue.

Best Practice

On projects with retainage (typically 5–10% held until final acceptance), proactively drive toward completion criteria. Every month of retainage outstanding is cash withheld from your firm with no benefit to you. Define "done" early in the project and hold to it.

Key Terms A–Z
AC (Actual Cost)
The cost actually incurred for work performed to date.
BAC (Budget at Completion)
Total authorized budget for the project. Your cost baseline.
Baseline
Approved scope, schedule, and cost plan. Formally changed only through change control.
CBS (Cost Breakdown Structure)
Hierarchical breakdown of project costs by category (labor, materials, subs, expenses).
Change Order
A formal document authorizing a change to scope, cost, or schedule. Required before performing out-of-scope work.
Contingency
Budget for identified risks that may occur. Released by the PM. Not for scope creep.
Control Account
The management unit at which cost and schedule performance are measured.
CPI (Cost Performance Index)
EV ÷ AC. Measures cost efficiency. Below 1.0 = over budget per unit of work.
Critical Path
The longest duration sequence of dependent activities. Determines the project end date.
EAC (Estimate at Completion)
Projected total cost at project completion. Updated periodically.
ETC (Estimate to Complete)
Expected cost to finish the remaining work. A forward-looking judgment, not a formula.
EV (Earned Value)
Budgeted cost of work actually completed. BAC × % complete.
Float / Slack
Time an activity can be delayed without delaying the project end date.
Forecast
A projection of future performance based on current data. EAC is the primary cost forecast.
Lump Sum Contract
Fixed price for a defined scope. Cost risk borne by the consultant.
Management Reserve
Budget for unknown unknowns. Held above the project level.
Milestone
A significant point in the schedule. Zero duration. Marks completion of a phase or major deliverable.
PV (Planned Value)
Budgeted cost of work planned to be done by the status date. The schedule baseline in cost terms.
RAG Status
Red/Amber/Green traffic light system for project health reporting.
Retainage
A percentage of contract value held by the client until final completion. Typically 5–10%.
Risk Register
A log of identified project risks with probability, impact, expected value, and response plans.
Scope Creep
Unauthorized expansion of project scope without adjustment to budget or schedule.
SPI (Schedule Performance Index)
EV ÷ PV. Measures schedule efficiency. Below 1.0 = behind schedule.
T&M (Time & Materials)
Contract type billed at actual rates. Cost risk borne by client.
VAC (Variance at Completion)
BAC − EAC. Projected final variance. Negative = projected overrun.
WBS (Work Breakdown Structure)
Hierarchical decomposition of total project scope into manageable components.
